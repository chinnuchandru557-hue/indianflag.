THE FLAG OF INDIA

The Indian national flag, known as the Tiranga, has a rich history that mirrors the country’s struggle for independence and its vision of unity. The earliest version appeared in 1906 at Calcutta’s Parsi Bagan Square, featuring green, yellow, and red stripes with symbols like the lotus, sun, crescent moon, and the words Vande Mataram. In 1907, freedom fighter Madam Bhikaji Cama carried a similar flag to Stuttgart, Germany, introducing India’s cause to the world. By 1917, Annie Besant and Bal Gangadhar Tilak introduced a flag during the Home Rule Movement, combining red and green stripes with the Union Jack, crescent, and stars, though it was criticized for not being fully representative. A major turning point came in 1921 when Pingali Venkayya designed a flag with red and green stripes, later modified by Gandhi to include white and a spinning wheel (charkha) symbolizing self-reliance. In 1931, the Indian National Congress adopted a tricolor of saffron, white, and green with the charkha, representing courage, peace, and prosperity. Finally, in 1947, the Constituent Assembly replaced the charkha with the Ashoka Chakra, a navy-blue wheel with 24 spokes symbolizing justice and progress. Adopted on 22 July 1947, the Tiranga became the emblem of independent India on 15 August 1947.

    Saffron (Top Band):

    • Meaning:

      Courage, sacrifice, renunciation, and selflessness.

    • philosphy:

      Leaders are reminded to rise above materialism and dedicate themselves to the nation.

    • Cultural Roots:

      Associated with Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism, where saffron represents purity and spirituality.

    White (Middle Band)

    • Meaning:

      Peace, truth, honesty, and harmony.

    • philosphy:

      Symbolizes the path of truth (Satya) and light, guiding India’s governance.

    • Cultural Roots:

      Represents purity and is linked to universal values across religions, including Christianity.

    Green (Bottom Band)

    • Meaning:

      Faith, fertility, prosperity, and growth.

    • philosphy:

      Reflects India’s agrarian foundation and respect for nature.

    • Associated with Islam and also symbolizes ecological balance.

    Ashoka Chakra (Navy Blue Wheel in the Center)

    • Meaning:

      Dharma (righteousness), justice, and eternal progress.

    • Design:

      24 spokes represent the 24 hours of the day — a reminder that India must keep moving forward without stagnation.

    • Origin:

      Taken from Emperor Ashoka’s Lion Capital at Sarnath, symbolizing law and moral order.
